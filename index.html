Health Promotion Board — Key Guidelines

HPB recommends at least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, combined with muscle-strengthening exercises at least 2 days per week.

  • Move More, Sit Less: Break up prolonged sitting every 30 minutes.
  • Healthy Plate Model: ½ vegetables/fruit, ¼ wholegrains, ¼ protein.
  • Muscle strength for seniors: Resistance and balance training recommended from age 40 onwards.
  • Fall prevention: HPB's Functional Screening for Seniors (FSS) emphasises balance, lower limb strength, and gait speed.

Understanding Your Body Type
Why genetics, nutrition, and training are deeply connected
✅ Evidence-Based ContentThe somatotype framework is a widely used educational model in fitness. Nutritional principles reflect real metabolic differences consistent with current dietetic guidelines. For personalised nutrition advice, consult a registered dietitian.

The Three Somatotypes

Developed by psychologist William Sheldon in the 1940s, somatotypes describe how individuals naturally store fat, build muscle, and respond to exercise. Most people are a blend, but understanding your dominant type is a useful starting point.

🔑 No body type is "better" than another. The goal is to work with your biology, not against it.

Ectomorph

Lean and long, fast metabolism. Struggles to gain weight or muscle. Narrow frame, small joints.

Endomorph

Wider frame, stores fat easily. Gains muscle well but also fat. Slower metabolism.

Mesomorph

Athletic and muscular naturally. Responds quickly to training. Gains and loses weight relatively easily.

🥗 Why Food Matters for Each Body Type

Ectomorph — The Hard Gainer

Ectomorphs burn through calories quickly. Their challenge is consuming enough food to support muscle growth. Without adequate nutrition, muscle development stalls and fatigue sets in.

  • Higher calorie intake: Aim for a calorie surplus to stimulate muscle growth.
  • Carbohydrate-rich diet: Complex carbs (brown rice, oats, whole grains) are essential fuel.
  • Frequent meals: Eating every 3–4 hours prevents muscle breakdown.
  • Adequate protein: 1.6–2.2g per kg bodyweight supports muscle repair.

💡 Approx. ratio: 50% carbs · 25% protein · 25% fat

Endomorph — The Easy Gainer

Endomorphs have a slower metabolism and greater tendency to store body fat. Carbohydrates are more readily converted and stored as fat.

  • Controlled carbohydrate intake: Focus on low-GI carbs (sweet potato, legumes, vegetables).
  • Higher protein focus: Protein increases satiety and preserves muscle.
  • Healthy fats: Avocado, nuts, olive oil, omega-3 fish.
  • Portion control: Track calorie intake to avoid surplus.

💡 Approx. ratio: 30% carbs · 35% protein · 35% fat

Mesomorph — The Natural Athlete

Mesomorphs are genetically predisposed to building muscle. However, without continued effort, muscle is still lost with age.

  • Balanced macronutrient ratio: Moderate balance of all three macronutrients.
  • Cycle nutrition with training: Higher carbs on training days, lower on rest days.
  • Consistent protein: Especially important for maintenance after age 40.

💡 Approx. ratio: 40% carbs · 30% protein · 30% fat

⚠️ The Ageing Factor

Regardless of body type, all adults experience a natural decline in muscle protein synthesis after age 30. Seniors should aim for 1.2–1.6g of protein per kg bodyweight daily.

Why Muscles Are So Important
Six life-changing reasons to invest in your muscle health today
✅ Evidence-Based ContentInformation consistent with guidelines from WHO, ACSM, and Singapore's HPB. For personalised medical advice, consult your doctor.

Muscles are far more than engines of movement — they are dynamic, metabolically active tissue that regulate key systems. Losing muscle (sarcopenia) accelerates disease, reduces independence, and shortens lifespan.

🩸

Blood Sugar Control

Muscles absorb glucose from the bloodstream, reducing blood sugar spikes

💉

Diabetes Prevention

Greater muscle mass means better insulin sensitivity and metabolic health

🚶

Mobility & Independence

Strong muscles keep you moving freely and living independently longer

⏳

Longevity

Muscle mass is one of the strongest predictors of a long, healthy life

🧠

Mental Health

Exercise triggers endorphins, reduces cortisol, and supports cognitive health

🦴

Joint & Bone Health

Muscles protect joints by absorbing force and stimulating bone density

1. 🩸 Blood Sugar Control

Skeletal muscle is responsible for up to 80% of glucose uptake after a meal. GLUT-4 transporter proteins activated during exercise pull glucose directly from the bloodstream — even independent of insulin.

🔑 Even a 10-minute walk after a meal can reduce post-meal blood sugar spikes by 20–30%.

2. 💉 Diabetes Prevention

Sarcopenia is an independent risk factor for Type 2 diabetes. Resistance training improves insulin sensitivity for up to 72 hours after a session — one of the most powerful diabetes prevention tools available.

3. 🚶 Mobility & Independence

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related deaths among older adults. Seniors with greater lower body strength have significantly reduced fall risk and are more likely to remain functionally independent well into their 80s and 90s.

🔑 The "Chair Stand Test" (sit-to-stand in 30 seconds) is one of the strongest predictors of long-term independence and all-cause mortality.

4. ⏳ Longevity

Research shows muscle mass index — not BMI — is one of the most reliable predictors of longevity. During illness or hospitalisation, the body draws on muscle protein to fuel immune function and recovery.

5. 🧠 Mental Health

Resistance and aerobic exercise trigger endorphins, serotonin, dopamine, and BDNF — a protein that supports neuroplasticity. Multiple meta-analyses show exercise is as effective as antidepressants for mild to moderate depression.

🔑 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week is associated with a 43% reduction in depression risk.

6. 🦴 Joint & Bone Health

Strong muscles act as shock absorbers for joints. Resistance training stimulates osteoblast activity and increases bone mineral density — the most effective non-pharmaceutical intervention against osteoporosis.

Decades of cumulative mechanical stress on a weakened musculoskeletal system

1. 🦴 Osteoporosis

After age 35, bone resorption gradually outpaces formation. A hip fracture in a senior carries a 20–30% mortality rate within one year — primarily from immobility complications.

  • Risk factors: Low calcium/vitamin D, smoking, alcohol, sedentary lifestyle, steroids
  • What helps: Weight-bearing exercise, resistance training, calcium 1,200mg/day, vitamin D, fall prevention

2. 💪 Sarcopenia

Adults lose approximately 3–8% of muscle mass per decade from age 30, accelerating sharply after 60. Low muscle mass leads to reduced metabolic rate → fat gain → more inflammation → more muscle loss.

  • What helps: Resistance training 2–3x/week, protein 1.2–1.6g/kg/day, leucine-rich foods, minimise sitting

3. 🔥 Arthritis

Pain leads to avoiding movement → muscle weakness → greater instability → more pain. Weak muscles worsen arthritic joints. Strengthening muscles around an arthritic joint significantly reduces pain.

🔑 "Motion is lotion" — gentle consistent movement lubricates cartilage through synovial fluid production.

4. 🛋️ Sedentary Lifestyle

Prolonged sitting increases lumbar disc pressure by up to 40% vs standing. Muscles not regularly used undergo disuse atrophy — they literally shrink. Break up sitting every 30 minutes.

5. ⬇️ Gravity & Postural Load

Decades of gravitational compression combined with weak muscles and poor posture causes disc degeneration, joint wear, and postural collapse. Most adults lose 2–3cm of height per decade after 50.

🔑 Strong postural muscles (deep spinal extensors, glutes, core) are your body's internal "anti-gravity suit."

It's Never Too Late to Start Training
The science of adaptation — how your body responds at any age
✅ Evidence-Based ContentBased on published research in exercise physiology and gerontology. Always consult a healthcare professional before beginning a new exercise programme.

The Great Myth: "I'm Too Old to Start"

Decades of research tell a completely different story. The human body retains a remarkable capacity for adaptation well into old age. Muscles can still grow, bones can still rebuild, and neural pathways can still be rewired.

🔬 A landmark study by Fiatarone et al. (1994) found nursing home residents aged 72–98 who completed 10 weeks of resistance training achieved an average 113% increase in muscle strength. Some had never formally exercised in their lives.

🦴 Why Bones Can Still Rebuild

Bone is living tissue constantly broken down and rebuilt. Wolff's Law: bone adapts to the loads it regularly experiences. When you stress a bone through weight-bearing or resistance exercise, new bone matrix is deposited.

  • Postmenopausal women who begin resistance training can increase bone mineral density by 1–3% over 12 months
  • Adequate calcium (1,000–1,200mg/day) and vitamin D are essential co-factors at any age

🔑 Bone responds to stimulus — not to age. A 70-year-old who begins regular weight-bearing exercise will have stronger bones 12 months later than one who doesn't.

💪 Why Muscle Can Still Grow

Sarcopenia is real but not irreversible. What changes with age is the rate of adaptation:

  • Anabolic resistance: Older muscles require 30–40g protein per meal (vs 20–25g in youth) to maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis
  • Slower recovery: 48–72 hours between sessions vs 24–48 hours in younger adults
  • Still works: Satellite cells that repair muscle remain functional and are activated by exercise

🔬 A meta-analysis of 49 studies found adults over 60 who performed resistance training gained an average of 1.1kg of lean muscle mass and improved strength by up to 30% over 6 months.

🧠 Neural Adaptation

A significant portion of early strength gains come from neural adaptation — the nervous system learns to recruit more motor units simultaneously. This neural plasticity remains robust into old age. Meaningful strength improvements happen within just 2–4 weeks of starting.

Active Ageing in Singapore — HPB Initiatives

  • Functional Screening for Seniors (FSS): Free community screenings assessing muscle strength, balance, and fall risk
  • National Steps Challenge: Island-wide campaign rewarding daily step milestones
  • Active Ageing Centres (AACs): Structured exercise, social activities, and health monitoring island-wide
  • Chronic Disease Management Programme: Subsidised exercise for seniors with diabetes, hypertension, and osteoporosis
